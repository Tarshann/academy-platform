# What Happens in a Performance Lab Cohort: Week by Week

*Published by The Academy | Gallatin, TN | March 2026*

---

Parents ask us all the time: "What exactly does my child do for 6 weeks?" It's a fair question. You're investing $245 and trusting us with your athlete three times a week. You deserve to know exactly what that looks like.

Here's the inside look at what happens from Day 1 to Day 42 in an Academy Performance Lab cohort.

## Week 1: Baseline Testing and Assessment

Every cohort begins the same way: we measure where your athlete is right now. No assumptions, no guesswork. We run each athlete through a standardized testing battery that includes pro agility (5-10-5 shuttle), 40-yard dash, vertical jump, broad jump, and movement quality assessments.

These numbers become your athlete's personal starting line. They also help our coaches identify individual strengths and areas for development, so we can tailor the group training to address what each athlete actually needs.

Parents receive their athlete's baseline scores. This is one of the things that sets The Academy apart: we don't just promise improvement, we give you the data to verify it.

## Weeks 2-3: Building the Foundation

With baseline data in hand, the real work begins. These sessions focus on movement quality, technique, and building the habits that will compound over the remaining weeks.

Athletes work through structured SAQ progressions: agility ladder sequences, cone work patterns, and reactive movement drills. Strength sessions introduce bodyweight foundations and age-appropriate resistance work. Every drill is coached with attention to form because speed built on bad mechanics is speed that breaks down under pressure.

This is also where athletes start to gel as a cohort. With only 6-8 athletes per group, coaches know every kid by name, know their goals, and know how to push them individually within the group setting.

## Weeks 4-5: Progressive Overload and Sport-Specific Work

By the midpoint, athletes have the movement foundation to handle increased intensity. Drills get faster. Rest periods get shorter. Competition elements are introduced so athletes learn to perform under pressure, not just in practice conditions.

Sport-specific skill integration begins here. The SAQ and strength work from earlier weeks gets applied to basketball-specific movements, football cutting patterns, and soccer agility sequences. Athletes start to see how the foundational work connects to what they do on game day.

This is typically when parents start noticing changes at home: their child moves differently, carries themselves with more confidence, and brings a sharper competitive edge to school practices and games.

## Week 6: Post-Testing and Results Review

The final week brings the moment of truth. Athletes run through the exact same testing battery from Week 1: pro agility, 40-yard dash, vertical jump, broad jump.

The numbers tell the story. Across cohorts, we consistently see measurable improvements in every category. Athletes gain fractions of a second on their agility and sprint times (which, in youth sports, translates to real competitive advantages). Jump heights increase. Movement quality visibly improves.

Parents receive a side-by-side comparison of baseline and post-test scores. These results belong to the family and can be shared with school coaches, club teams, or anyone else who wants to see objective evidence of development.

## What Parents See Along the Way

Beyond the testing data, here's what families typically notice during a 6-week cohort:

Your athlete will talk about training differently than they talk about practice. They'll push themselves harder because the small-group environment creates accountability. They'll develop friendships with other serious athletes. And they'll carry the confidence of measurable improvement into every other sport and activity.
