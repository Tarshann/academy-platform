# 5 Signs Your Young Athlete Needs Structured Training

*Published by The Academy | Gallatin, TN | March 2026*

---

Your kid plays sports. They go to practice, play in games, maybe do a camp over the summer. They're active, competitive, and love being on the field or court. But something feels like it's not clicking. They're working hard, yet the improvement isn't matching the effort.

Here are five signs that your young athlete might benefit from structured athletic training beyond regular team practice.

## 1. They're Fast in a Straight Line but Can't Change Direction

This is one of the most common things we see at The Academy. An athlete who can outrun everyone in a sprint but gets beaten to every loose ball, can't shake a defender, or gets caught flat-footed on defense.

Straight-line speed is only one piece of athleticism. Game speed requires the ability to accelerate, decelerate, cut, and re-accelerate in fractions of a second. That's agility, and it's trained differently than sprinting.

SAQ (Speed, Agility, Quickness) training addresses this directly with cone drills, lateral movement patterns, and reactive change-of-direction work. It's the difference between being fast and being *quick*, and in youth sports, quick wins.

## 2. They're Strong but Uncoordinated

Some kids are naturally powerful. They can muscle through situations on the field. But power without coordination leads to wasted energy, injury risk, and diminishing returns as competition gets better.

Coordination is about teaching the body to move efficiently. It's footwork, balance, body control, and timing. These aren't things most team practices teach explicitly. Structured training breaks down movement into teachable progressions so athletes build coordination alongside their natural strength.

## 3. They Excel at One Sport but Struggle in Others

If your child dominates in basketball but can't keep up in soccer, or shines in football but looks lost on the baseball diamond, it usually means they've developed sport-specific skills without building a broad athletic base.

This isn't a problem today, but it becomes one. Athletes with narrow skill sets hit ceilings earlier. They're more prone to overuse injuries. And they miss out on the cross-sport transfer that makes elite athletes so adaptable.

Multi-sport training builds the underlying athleticism: speed in all directions, reactive quickness, dynamic balance, and functional strength that makes every sport easier.

## 4. They're Losing Confidence After Tough Games

This one is subtle but important. When a young athlete starts dreading practice, making excuses to skip games, or getting visibly frustrated during competition, it's often because they sense they're falling behind but don't know how to fix it.

More practice in the same sport rarely solves a confidence problem. What works is giving the athlete measurable proof of improvement. When a kid sees their agility time drop by half a second or their vertical jump increase by two inches, that's concrete evidence that they're getting better. That evidence builds the internal confidence that shows up in competition.

At The Academy, baseline and post-testing give athletes and parents objective proof of growth. We've seen countless times how a kid who walks in unsure of themselves walks out six weeks later standing taller, competing harder, and believing in their ability.

## 5. They've Hit a Performance Plateau

Your athlete has been playing for years. They practice consistently. But the improvement curve has flattened. They're not getting worse, but they're not getting noticeably better either.

Plateaus happen when the training stimulus stays the same. Team practice teaches sport tactics and team systems, but it doesn't progressively overload an athlete's speed, agility, or strength. That requires structured periodization: increasing intensity and complexity over time in a planned way.

A 6-week Performance Lab cohort is specifically designed to break through plateaus. The structured progression, combined with small group sizes and individualized coaching attention, introduces the new stimulus that team practice alone can't provide.

## What to Look For in a Training Program

If any of these signs resonate, here's what to look for when evaluating training options: objective testing to measure progress (not just coach opinion), small group sizes for individualized attention, multi-sport methodology that builds transferable athleticism, qualified coaches with youth development experience, and a structured curriculum rather than random workouts.
